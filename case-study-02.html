Product Design for a B2B SaaS Website Builder

UX UI DESIGN

Role

UX UI Designer

Team

Product Owner, Engineers UX Designer

Duration

16 months

Running a niche website marketing service, my client was looking to develop a website builder derived from their current software.

Our primary objective was to design a website builder tailored for churches, allowing them to effortlessly create and manage professional websites without requiring coding or design skills.

I remotely collaborated with a cross-functional team in Seattle, overseeing all aspects of design — from defining the product's functionality to shaping its appearance and user experience.

Discovery & Planning

Stakeholder and customer discovery interviews

I conducted remote interviews with stakeholders and the Product Owner to thoroughly grasp the project's context. Through these sessions, I gathered insights into their goals, expectations, limitations and challenges, taking into account factors like the project not being the company's primary focus and the Product Owner being involved in multiple other projects simultaneously.

We want a product so intuitive that it leaves no room for questions

  • Able to drop price point
  • Remove non-essential features
  • Minimize support calls
  • Reduce development overhead

Gathering of system & technical requirements

There was regular collaboration with the development team to understand technical requirements and constraints, ensuring our design aligned seamlessly with the tech stack. Additionally, we collaborated on the database structure and information architecture to maintain consistency and coherence across the entire system.

Reseach

Competitor Analysis

I researched best practices of website builders like WebFlow and Wix, and also tested software like Dreamweaver. This helped me to identify common features and design trends, and better understand user expectations. It also helped me to identify gaps where competitors may have fallen short so we could differentiate our product.

User Research

I conducted guerrilla user research to keep within the budget constraints because of its flexibility and speed. This involved interviews with key stakeholders, account managers and support team members. They were expert system users with firsthand insights from customer queries and support tickets. I also did online research about church marketing needs and types of roles typically needed in running a church organisation.

Functional Analysis

Fully understanding the existing system was crucial because we needed to redesign component features in the new software. I documented each component, outlined necessary functions, defined core user stories, and worked through the user tasks. This process provided insights into the current system, pinpointed areas for enhancement, and guided the redesign.

SYNTHESIZE & DEFINE

By synthesizing user research, I crafted proto personas, problem statements and user stories.

This allowed us to focus on users with distinct characteristics beneficial to the team. Understanding their individual goals and preferences allowed us to develop a more engaging and user-centric product. Additionally, it helped us account for variations in their onboarding experiences.

With approved personas, I defined their problem statements and user stories, crucial for team consensus on primary requirements. Using these user stories as a testing guideline, we could ensure that we met our users' defined tasks and goals in the prototype phase.

UX DESIGN

Information architecture and user flows

We had to organise and categorize information in a way that made sense for our users and ensuring consistency across the product was challenging. I presented various configurations using sitemaps and user flows, aiming to keep categories clear and establish a cohesive labeling and taxonomy for a well-defined mental model.

Sketching and wireframes

Paper sketching to quickly generate and explore a wide range of ideas. They included various concepts like layouts, navigation ideas, component layouts and the flow of user interactions and onboarding.

After settling on some ideas, I expanded on selected concepts using wireframes and medium-fidelity prototypes. These visuals served as a shared reference point during discussions, reducing the chances of misunderstandings.

VISUAL AND UI DESIGN

Transforming mockups into interactive prototypes.

Collaborating with the Product Owner, I shared design concepts, like mood boards, to understand their preferences. Despite no specific branding instructions, we swiftly picked colors, avoiding prolonged deliberation. I showcased ideas through high-fidelity mockups, refining them based on feedback. Eventually, I developed interactive prototypes and a design library using XD.

Simple & easy-to-navigate interface

An intuitive site editor with a clean design, enabling users to effortlessly add pages, sections, choose pre-designed layouts, and start customization with minimal clicks.

Add sections to a page with a single click

Letting users easily add ready-made sections to their page, preview them, and find sections that match their content.

Customize theming to match your brand

Creating an easy way for users to pick a theme from various pre-made designs, tailored to their industry or preferences, and then customize it as they like.

Preview and apply changes immediately

Adding a simple click-through preview feature for users to see layouts instantly and apply them easily with a single click, making customization effortless.

Gathering design specifications for developers

XD prototypes, featuring design specs, a component library, and exportable elements, were shared with developers. User flows for crucial tasks highlighted in user stories were outlined, aiding developers in grasping the precise user journey. Animations and key interactions were communicated through comments in XD.

TESTING & VALIDATION

Regular testing with XD prototypes

We regularly tested XD prototypes, closely involving the development team and Product Owner. This iterative approach helped us incorporate feedback, ensuring alignment with expectations. Since the product was still in development, testing and validation were limited to in-house users and stakeholders.

TAKEAWAY

Taking the initiative can be important

Currently, Aqoya is still in development as the team had to prioritize other projects. However, the backend components that have been developed are already in use internally.

Up to now, the time on task and task success rates have significantly enhanced the experiences of in-house developers and designers. This improvement is attributed to both upgraded technologies and enhanced usability of the components.

This project has emphasized the significance of balancing business requirements and user needs. It also taught me the importance of taking initiative, especially when a client is occupied with managing their own business and may not always be readily available.
